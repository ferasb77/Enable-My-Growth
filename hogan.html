Hogan Assessment

Assessment without context creates false confidence.

Scores become meaningful only when examined alongside role demands, organizational context, leadership challenges, and individual experience. Assessment should improve judgment — not replace it.

What Hogan Measures

Three lenses. One coherent picture.

HPI
Personality Inventory

How a person typically behaves when at their best — the strengths and characteristics they bring to their everyday performance and leadership.

HDS
Development Survey

The behaviors that tend to emerge under pressure, stress, or disengagement — the patterns most likely to derail a leader's effectiveness or relationships.

MVPI
Motives, Values & Preferences

What a person fundamentally wants from work — the values and motivators that drive satisfaction, commitment, and the environments in which they are likely to thrive.

Applications

Where assessment creates the most value

Executive Development

Individual assessment and debrief for senior leaders — understanding strengths, risks, and the conditions that bring out the best and worst in their leadership.

Leadership Selection

Using assessment to inform — not determine — selection decisions. Understanding how a candidate's profile aligns with the specific demands of a role and organization.

Succession Planning

Identifying development priorities and readiness considerations for leaders being prepared for greater responsibility.

Team Effectiveness

Examining how individual profiles interact at the team level — understanding collective strengths, blind spots, and cultural tendencies.

Coaching & Development

Using assessment as a structured starting point for development conversations — providing language and evidence for patterns that might otherwise be difficult to examine.

My Approach

I do not view assessment scores as explanations.

In the wrong hands, psychometric data becomes a new form of premature explanation — personality labels that substitute for genuine understanding. The HPI does not explain why a leader struggled in a particular role. The HDS does not tell us whether a derailer will manifest, or under what conditions.

What assessment data does is provide a structured starting point — a set of hypotheses that need to be examined in context, not accepted as conclusions.

"Assessment should improve judgment, not replace it."

Every debrief I conduct begins from this position. The scores are a starting point for a conversation — not a verdict about a person. The most valuable outcome of a well-conducted assessment is not a profile summary. It's a leader who understands themselves more clearly and can use that understanding to make better decisions.
