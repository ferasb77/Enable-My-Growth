Why Assessment Scores Are Not Explanations

One of the quickest ways to misuse an assessment is to treat it as more certain than its creators intended.

This is not a criticism of assessment. Quite the opposite. It is a defense of it.

Assessment tools are designed to identify tendencies, patterns, preferences, and likely risks. They provide valuable information that would otherwise remain difficult to observe. Problems begin when a score stops being treated as information and starts being treated as an explanation.

A person receives a result. A behavior is observed. The score is recruited as the reason. The conversation ends.

Yet assessments were never designed to eliminate inquiry. They were designed to improve it.

Description tells us what may be present.
Explanation attempts to tell us why something happened.

Most assessments are considerably stronger at the first task than the second.

Consider how often people encounter statements such as:

"They reacted that way because they scored high on Bold." "They avoid conflict because their Adjustment is lower." "They struggle with structure because their Prudence score is low." "They are highly competitive because of their Ambition score."

The observations may contain elements of truth. The problem is not the score. The problem is the certainty. The score has quietly shifted from describing a tendency to explaining a behavior. Those are different tasks.

A personality assessment can suggest that an individual may be more likely to seek visibility, challenge authority, pursue achievement, value structure, or tolerate ambiguity. What it cannot reliably tell us is why a specific behavior occurred on a specific day in a specific situation. Human behavior is rarely that simple.

People do not operate as assessment reports. They operate inside environments. They respond to incentives. They react to consequences. They navigate expectations. They manage relationships. They adapt to circumstances.

The same individual can appear remarkably different across different contexts.

A person who appears highly confident in one role may appear cautious in another.

A person who appears reserved in one team may appear outspoken in another.

A person who appears collaborative in one environment may appear competitive in another.

The underlying tendencies may remain relatively stable. The behavior may not.

This is where assessment often becomes misunderstood. Many people expect assessments to function like explanations. In reality, they function more like maps. A map can help identify possible routes. It cannot tell you everything that happened during the journey. Two individuals may arrive at the same behavior through entirely different paths.

This distinction becomes especially important in leadership assessment. The temptation is understandable — the reports contain rich information, patterns emerge, themes become visible, insights are generated. The assessment begins to feel explanatory.

Yet some of the most valuable assessment conversations occur when the report creates questions rather than answers. An elevated score becomes an invitation to explore. A lower score becomes an area of curiosity. A pattern becomes a hypothesis rather than a conclusion. The report helps guide the conversation rather than replace it.

Partly because explanations feel satisfying. A completed explanation creates closure. The uncertainty disappears. The answer appears available. Unfortunately, closure and understanding are not the same thing.

An assessment score can easily become a convenient explanation for behavior that has not yet been adequately examined. The score receives credit for causes that may belong to context, incentives, relationships, expectations, culture, experience, or circumstance. The explanation feels complete. The understanding remains incomplete.

The strongest assessors rarely treat reports as verdicts. They treat them as evidence. Evidence deserves consideration. Evidence deserves scrutiny. Evidence deserves context.

A score can identify a tendency.

A score can highlight a potential strength.

A score can reveal a likely risk.

What it cannot do is explain human behavior in isolation. That responsibility belongs to interpretation. And interpretation begins where the report ends.

The value of assessment has never been its ability to eliminate uncertainty. Its value lies in helping us navigate uncertainty more intelligently.

The moment a score becomes the explanation, that advantage begins to disappear.

The report has not become more insightful. It has simply become more convenient.

And convenience has a habit of ending inquiry precisely when inquiry is needed most.
