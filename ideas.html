Ideas

A developing body of work

These are not finished positions. They are lines of inquiry — recurring questions about leadership, behavior, and the explanations we accept too quickly.

01

Core Idea

Conditions Before Conviction

Conviction is often treated as a prerequisite for action. What if conviction is the outcome of aligned conditions instead?

Leaders are frequently advised to act boldly, commit decisively, and project certainty. The implicit assumption is that conviction is something a person either has or lacks — an internal resource they can summon through discipline or will.

This framing creates a problem. When conviction doesn't arrive on schedule, people either force commitment before the conditions support it — increasing the likelihood of poor decisions — or they mistake the absence of conviction for a character flaw rather than a signal worth examining.

A more useful question is: what conditions reliably precede genuine conviction? And if those conditions are absent, is the answer to push harder — or to understand what's missing?

02

Core Idea

Context Before Explanation

We routinely explain behavior through the person before examining the conditions that made the behavior reasonable.

In organizations, this appears constantly. A leader underperforms and the explanation defaults to personality, motivation, or fit. A team struggles and the narrative settles on individuals. A decision fails and someone is identified as having lacked the judgment required.

These explanations are not necessarily wrong. But they are frequently incomplete — and accepted too quickly, before the conditions shaping the behavior have been seriously examined.

When we misattribute cause, we apply the wrong remedies. Assessment becomes judgment rather than insight. Development addresses the wrong problem. And the conditions that generated the behavior remain unchanged, ready to shape whoever comes next.

03

Core Idea

The Inner Conversation

The assumptions we rarely examine often have more influence than the conclusions we consciously defend.

Every leader carries an internal narrative — about who they are, what people expect of them, what risks are acceptable, and what counts as failure. These narratives are not always visible, even to the person holding them.

They shape what information gets noticed, which options get considered seriously, and how uncertainty is tolerated. They are not character flaws. They are the residue of experience — useful in the contexts that formed them, potentially limiting in the contexts that require something different.

The inner conversation is where much of the real decision-making happens. Making it more visible — more examinable — is one of the most valuable things a leader can do.

04

Core Idea

Decision Integrity

The quality of decisions is constrained by the quality of the conditions surrounding them.

Organizations invest heavily in selecting and developing people with good judgment. They invest far less in building the conditions in which good judgment can actually operate.

Information quality, psychological safety, time available for reflection, clarity of priorities, the degree to which dissent is tolerated — these factors shape the decisions that get made, regardless of the quality of the people making them.

Decision integrity asks a different set of questions: not only who is deciding, but what conditions surrounded the decision. Was the information complete? Were alternatives genuinely considered? Was pressure to conclude premature? Understanding these conditions — before and after — is how decision quality actually improves.
