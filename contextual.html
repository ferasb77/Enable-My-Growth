Context before explanation

Scores describe patterns. They do not explain a person.

The same assessment pattern can mean something different under different role demands, relationships, pressures, and organizational conditions.

Contextual helps assessors slow the movement from score to story. It brings assessment evidence, role requirements, situational pressures, and competing interpretations into one disciplined preparation process—before a confident explanation hardens.

The Contextual promise

Contextual will not interpret the person for you.

It will help you distinguish evidence from inference.

It will help you examine what the scores may mean under the conditions that matter.

The assessor remains responsible for the judgment, conversation, and ethical use of the evidence.

The preparation method

From assessment data to a more defensible debrief position.

A deliberate sequence helps the assessor widen the frame without losing the evidence.

01

Establish the context

Clarify the role, purpose, environment, and questions shaping the assessment.

02

Read the evidence

Examine patterns across strengths, risks, values, and relevant scale interactions.

03

Test interpretations

Consider alternative meanings and the conditions under which each may be plausible.

04

Prepare the conversation

Develop hypotheses, probes, tensions, and questions rather than fixed conclusions.

05

Exercise judgment

Use professional expertise to decide what deserves attention in the debrief.

What it helps you examine

The conditions that change what assessment evidence may mean.

Role demands

What the work actually requires—and which tendencies may help or hinder performance.

Operating context

The pressures, constraints, incentives, and relationships surrounding the person.

Strength patterns

How everyday tendencies may combine, compensate for one another, or create trade-offs.

Risk under pressure

What may become more visible when strengths are overused or self-management weakens.

Values and motives

What may shape attention, satisfaction, culture fit, and the meaning attached to success.

Competing explanations

What else could reasonably account for the pattern before one interpretation is privileged.

For qualified practitioners

Preparation support—not automated assessment.

Contextual is designed for assessors preparing consequential feedback and development conversations.

It supports structured examination; it does not replace certification, professional competence, informed consent, or direct dialogue with the participant.

Professional judgment remains central. Outputs may be incomplete or mistaken. Assessment information must be handled in accordance with applicable permissions, privacy requirements, and professional standards.
